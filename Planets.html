What are Planets?

Planets are large celestial bodies that orbit a star and do not produce their own light. In our solar system, all planets revolve around the Sun due to its strong gravitational pull. To be classified as a planet, a body must orbit the Sun, be nearly spherical in shape due to its gravity, and have cleared its orbital path of other objects. Our solar system has eight recognized planets, divided into two main groups: the inner rocky planets and the outer giant planets. Each planet is unique in size, composition, atmosphere, and temperature.

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in the solar system. Because it is so close to the Sun, it completes one orbit in just 88 Earth days. It has a rocky surface filled with craters, similar to Earth’s Moon. Mercury has almost no atmosphere, which means it cannot retain heat. As a result, temperatures can rise above 400°C during the day and drop below −170°C at night. Despite being closest to the Sun, it is not the hottest planet.

Venus

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and is often called Earth’s twin because of its similar size and structure. However, its environment is extremely harsh. Venus has a very thick atmosphere made mostly of carbon dioxide, which creates a powerful greenhouse effect. This traps heat and makes Venus the hottest planet, with surface temperatures high enough to melt lead. The planet rotates very slowly and in the opposite direction compared to most planets.

Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet to support life. It has a protective atmosphere composed mainly of nitrogen and oxygen, which shields living organisms from harmful solar radiation. Earth’s surface contains continents, oceans, mountains, and forests. The presence of liquid water is one of the most important factors that make life possible. Earth also has one natural satellite, the Moon, which influences tides and stabilizes the planet’s tilt.

Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and is known as the Red Planet because of the iron-rich dust that covers its surface. It has seasons similar to Earth due to its tilted axis. Mars is home to Olympus Mons, the largest volcano in the solar system, and Valles Marineris, one of the largest canyon systems. Although Mars is cold and dry today, evidence suggests that liquid water once flowed on its surface.

Jupiter

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest in the solar system. It is a gas giant composed mainly of hydrogen and helium. Jupiter does not have a solid surface like Earth. One of its most famous features is the Great Red Spot, a massive storm that has lasted for over 300 years. Jupiter has a very strong magnetic field and more than 90 known moons, including some of the largest moons in the solar system.

Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and is best known for its spectacular ring system. These rings are made up of billions of ice and rock particles. Like Jupiter, Saturn is a gas giant with no solid surface. It is less dense than water, meaning it would float if placed in a large enough ocean. Saturn has more than 80 known moons, some of which are believed to have subsurface oceans.

Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and is classified as an ice giant. It appears blue-green due to methane gas in its atmosphere. Unlike most planets, Uranus rotates on its side, with its axis tilted at about 98 degrees. This unusual tilt causes extreme seasonal variations that last for many years. Uranus has faint rings and numerous moons

Neptune

Neptune is the eighth and farthest planet from the Sun. It is another ice giant and is similar in size and composition to Uranus. Neptune is known for its deep blue color and extremely strong winds, which are among the fastest in the solar system. Despite being so far from the Sun, Neptune has a very dynamic atmosphere with visible storms.
